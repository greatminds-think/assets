Building Habits

Introduction into building habits, why we do it, the benfits

Stack your habits

We build your new habit by pairing it with something you already do and then nudge you to practice at a time that’s convenient and repeatable.

Your brain builds a network of ‘neurons’ to support things you do regularly i.e. your habits. You have habits you do every day without even thinking about it - for example, brushing your teeth.

You can take advantage of these existing habits, (and their neuron networks) to build new ones.

Read more about habit stacking

Make it easy

We give you time based exercises so that they fit into your day and you can build your new habit no matter how motivated you feel.

Actions are easier to do and maintain when they are as frictionless as possible. Human beings are naturally lazy, so the easier something is the more likely we are to do it.

Ease can come in a number of ways; time, money, physical effort, mental effort and routine. We aim to reduce all of these factors, so you’re more likely to take these actions.

Read more about ability

Remind yourself why

We resurface your personal reasons for improving your creativity so you are motivated to continue.

No matter how easy something is to do, most of us will only do it if it helps us to achieve something. By reminding you of your reasons for improving your creative thinking, we aim to bring your intentions closer to actions.

The best habits are formed when you can see the clear links between a goal and an action.

Read more about the intention gap

Celebrate progress

We all want to celebrate success either because it satisfies us or teaches us something. Satisfaction rewards are easier to understand - we’ve all felt something that brings us contentment, both in the short and long term.

Choose what to do

We give you the option to pick exercises that appeal to you so that you are the master of your own learning.

It’s key to make sure any new habits are reasonable so you can maintain them. Key to this is being able to decide what actions you want to take on a day or a week, so they can fit into your life. For example, James Clear challenges you to scale down your habit to be something you can do in two minutes.

For example, if you want to regularly practice yoga, start by getting your mat out of the cupboard and rolling it out. This creates a narrative where the habit is established before it can be improved.

Read more about the two minute rule

Find accountability

Connect with other like-minded individuals to help each other achieve your goals

We’ve all done it - signed up for the 10K and then wanted to drop out, but you couldn’t because you’d already told people you were doing it. It’s about feeling like you have a duty to others so you’re less likely to quit.

Accountability is a great tool for making and breaking habits. It works best when it comes from the outside - i.e. from someone else.
